1 Financial Instruments

At Jane Street, we trade many different types of financial instruments. For this competition, we will focus on a few of the basic types.

1.1 Stocks

Many companies have securities that you can trade, "shares", which represent ownership of a fraction of the company.

1.2 ADRs

Sometimes investors want to be able to buy and sell stocks of foreign companies on US exchanges. This can be achieved by trading a receipt called an ADR (American Depository Receipt), issued by a US bank. An ADR can be converted to the foreign stock by bringing it to the issuer.

1.3 Currencies

Other financial instruments are bought and sold with currencies, such as US Dollars (USD), British Pounds (GBP), or Hong Kong Dollars (HKD). Many currencies can also be traded in terms of others, so for example trading USDHKD lets you buy or sell US dollars using Hong Kong dollars. Some currencies trade freely, while others have fixed relationships to each other.

Each unit of a currency pair you trade today is for 10000 of the base currency, so if you buy 1 USDHKD, you will be receiving 10000 USD.

2 Orders

In the most basic sense, trading is buying and selling securities. There are different kinds of orders that one might send to an exchange, the most common being a limit order. A limit order is an instruction to buy or sell a security at a particular price (the "limit").

The parameters that specify a limit order are:

  • The name of the security you want to trade
  • Whether you want to buy or sell it
  • The "worst" price you're willing to trade at (i.e. the highest price if you're buying, or the lowest price if you're selling).
  • How many shares / contracts of the security you want to trade.

A "bid" price is the price specified by a buy order, and an "offer" or "ask" price is the price specified by a sell order.

Finally, someone comes along and sends a sell order for 30 shares at a price of $995. This can trade with the buy orders. Which orders get "filled"? First the 10-share buy order gets fully filled, as it was on the exchange first (this is called "price-time priority": the orders are sorted first by price, and then by age). The owner of the buy order gets a message from the exchange telling them how many of their shares traded (all 10 in this case). Now there are 20 shares left from the 30-share sell order. Next, the 100-share buy order gets partially filled with the remaining 20 shares. The owner of this buy order gets a message from the exchange as well, telling them 20 of their shares traded. And of course, the owner of the sell order also gets a message (or possibly multiple messages) about their fills.

3.1 Price improvement

Any given trade is always between an order that was previously on the book (the resting order), and one that comes in and never gets entered into the book.

The price of a trade is always that of the resting order. For example, if there is a resting order to buy for 80, and someone sends an order to sell for 70, a trade goes up at the price of 80.

4 The business of market making

Market making is one way that firms participate on the financial markets. The purpose of market making is to act as an intermediary for other people's trading. For example, suppose Alice wants to buy some shares, and Bob wants to sell some. It seems good for them to trade with each other, but if we require that they meet at the exchange at the same time, this is not likely to be efficient. If this were really how markets worked, you might be afraid to invest. Who knows whether you'd be lucky enough to find a buyer when it came to sell? And if you did find a counterparty, how would you agree on a fair price?

Market makers are always willing to buy and always willing to sell. They have some conception of how much a security is worth right now (its "fair value"), but no long term interest in owning the security. Market makers are always willing to buy at slightly below the fair value and sell at slightly above it. You can think of market making a bit like running a supermarket: you are simply the link between the sellers of goods (wholesale producers) and the buyers (shoppers). You charge some premium on your wholesale purchase price in order to make a profit.

Unlike a supermarket however, a market maker can sell securities that they don't currently own, called "short selling", and end up with a negative position (number of the security they own). You should try to keep your position close to 0 in order to limit your exposure to large price moves, on which you don't really have a long-term opinion.

It's important to note that it's wrong to think of trading as strictly a zero-sum game. In particular, there will be other participants in the etc market beyond the bots you create. Some of these might act like "investors" -- people just wishing to buy (or sell) securities, who want to keep that position for a long time, putting capital at risk in order to earn a return. They're not very sensitive to the price, since they mostly just trust that the market has the fair price for the security right now. So they're likely to buy or sell on whatever market they see. Other participants are competing with you – they have a rough idea of what they think the security is worth, and they'll try to buy below that and sell above it.

5 Some basic trading strategies

The basic strategy is:

  • Have some idea of a fair value for each security
  • Attempt to buy the security for less than fair, and sell it for more than fair.

5.1 Fair values

How might you come up with a fair value in the first place? Since markets (both in the real world and in this competition) tend to be fairly efficient most of the time, a good starting point is that fair value is probably between the best bid and best offer; half-way between these ("mid") is usually a good starting place for fair.

There are some classes of securities for which coming up with a better fair is relatively straightfoward. For example, as mentioned above, you can convert an ADR to its underlying security for a fee, so if you have an adr FOO and an underlying stock BAR, and know the conversion rate between their currencies, it should be fairly clear how to get the price for one in terms of anotehr.

For other securities, it is less obvious what you might do. In general, predicting whether a stock will go up or down is hard, so it is unlikely you will be able to make money by betting on a stock going up or down. That said, it can still be possible to make money by providing liquidity in these stocks, but we we will give no further hints in this section :)

5.2 Trading from your fair value

It may seem that all the trickery is in coming up with a fair, and indeed it is possible to do some very complex things in that space. But it's also not obvious how to trade around it. Let's look at some basic ideas.

If you think a financial security is worth exactly $100, it is impossible to make money by sending buy or sell orders at exactly $100. Obviously, you'll want to send buy orders below $100 and sell orders above $100. The difference between the price of your order or fill and your fair value is called the edge. More precisely: 

 edge = dir_sign * (fair value - order price)
 where dir_sign = 1 for buying and -1 for selling

The way to think about edge is that it is the profit you earn by completing that trade.

So given a fair value, where should you send orders? Let's look at two ideas:

5.3 Pennying

You want to earn the most edge you can. Pennying is the idea of looking at the current best bid and offer, and sending orders the minimum amount "inside" that. I.e. you send a buy order at [current best bid + 1] and/or a sell order at [current best offer - 1].

This kind of strategy is an example of "providing liquidity". You place orders on the book, and when price-insensitive investors come along to trade, they find your orders already sitting there, and trade against them.

5.4 Taking

On rare occasions, you might believe that the fair value is outside the current best bid and offer, in which case you might want to trade with the current market. For example, if you think [fair value] > [current best offer], you might send a buy order at [current best offer] to trade with the offer.

This kind of strategy is often referred to as "taking liquidity".

WARNING: You generally need to have a good idea of fair value to make money by taking liquidity! Remember markets are generally fairly efficient most of the time!
